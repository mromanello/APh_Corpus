Both the first and second prologues to the « Hecyra » have much to tell us about the ways in which the traditional stage drama of Terence 's day defined itself in relation to the rival counterdiversions and popular attractions of 2nd cent.
B. C. Rome.
What is at stake in the « Hecyra » prologues is theater in its entirety, as both a literary and a performative experience.
While Terence sustains the picture of his play as unfairly challenged by valueless attractions, « Theater » carves out for itself a distinctive terrain of unsurpassable intellectuality.
At issue is the very ranking of the « ludi scaenici » within the miscellany of entertainments on offer.
