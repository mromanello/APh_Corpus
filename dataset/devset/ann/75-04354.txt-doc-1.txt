At Miles 692-694 the old man Periplectomenus reels off a list of female « psychics ».
Women are well-attested as witches and sorceresses, but not as soothsayers, or interpreters of dreams or prodigies.
We should not be too quick, however, to dismiss these diviners as fictional.
Stylistic considerations suggest we should take some of them seriously, and the same holds true for the picture painted by Plautus (Curculio 229-279) and Ennius (fr. 134 Jocelyn) of informal private divination.
In a period that is not well documented, Periplectomenus ' casual reference offers a valuable glimpse into little-known activities of middle- and lower-class women.
