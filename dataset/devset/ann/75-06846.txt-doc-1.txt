Forty years after the pioneering work of T. B. L. Webster on the interdisciplinary study of tragedy and its monuments (=> APh 34, p. 262, under Webster, 2nd title), a kaleidoscopic view of the present state of that study may be attempted.
New evidence from Roman sarcophagi and Campanian wall painting allows us provisionally to map the occurrence of tragic themes in Italy.
The early popularity of Euripides outside Athens and his superior popularity in later antiquity should be stressed.
A revival of interest in the 2nd cent.
A. D. transformed tragic acting.
Tragedies are mostly depicted as myths, not as seen on the stage, while in stage depictions of tragedy the widest reponse was to acting, not to subject matter, as evidenced by the emphasis on servant parts in clay figurines and in masks.
